# Answer

Based on the provided evidence, the patient's presentation of resistant hypertension, hypokalemia (2.9 mmol/L), metabolic alkalosis (bicarbonate 31 mmol/L), suppressed renin, and elevated aldosterone is diagnostic of **Liddle syndrome** (also known as pseudohyperaldosteronism) [E1]. This rare autosomal dominant disorder is caused by a gain-of-function mutation in the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC) in the distal tubule and collecting duct, leading to constitutive sodium reabsorption and volume expansion [E2]. Although the clinical picture mimics primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's syndrome) with suppressed renin and high aldosterone, Liddle syndrome is distinguished by the fact that the elevated aldosterone is a secondary response to volume expansion rather than primary overproduction, and crucially, the condition does not respond to spironolactone because the defect lies in the sodium channel itself, not the mineralocorticoid receptor [E1]. The diagnosis is confirmed via genetic analysis of ENaC sequences, and treatment requires potassium-sparing diuretics that directly inhibit ENaC, such as amiloride or triamterene, rather than aldosterone antagonists [E1].
